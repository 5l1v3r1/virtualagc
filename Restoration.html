What is "The Restoration"?

"The Restoration" is an attempt to power up and run one of the original Apollo Guidance Computers.  I'm told (in 2019) that there is presently no other AGC in operation in the world.

  

Specifically, this is an attempt to operate one particular Block II AGC:

Part number: 2003100-071
Serial number:  (Raytheon) 14
Owner:  Jimmie Loocke

I have not been in contact with Jimmie (I think), but according to online information, he worked in Project Apollo as a thermal vacuum test technician for LTA-8 (Lunar Test Article), among other things.  He was thus in a good position years later to recognize the AGC when he saw it in a warehouse of junk that was intended to be scrapped for recycling purposes.  After rescuing the AGC from its fate, the idea was eventually conceived of actually restoring it and operating it.

Jimmie Loocke (right), Charlie Duke (center)

While this AGC never flew a mission, the restoration team believes it was indeed used in the vacuum testing of LTA-8.  This seems like a reasonable hypothesis since LTA-8's G&N (Guidance & Navigation) system, #602, did use an AGC p/n 2003100-071.  Moreover, we have no record of that AGC model having been used for any other purpose.  On the other hand, there are several things about this particular AGC that we wouldn't expect if the team's hypothesis were literally true:

  • One of the circuit modules in the AGC is dated 1970, and thus would not even have been built yet when LTA-8 testing was ongoing. 
  • Some modules have not been "potted" — i.e., not filled up with sealant — which is not something that would be likely for a unit used in environmental testing.
  • Module B11, the CURRENT SWITCH MODULE, has the wrong p/n:  it should be 2003026-011 — indeed, all known configurations of AGC 2003100 used 2003026-011 — but it is actually 2003026-021 instead.  In point of fact, 2003026-021 was used in some AGC 2003200 configurations rather than in AGC 2003100.  Such a substitution would be very unlikely if qualification testing was taking place.

Thus if this AGC had indeed originally been used in LTA-8 vacuum testing, a number of its modules must have been swapped out later and aren't original to the unit ... which is certainly a possibility and does not disprove the team's hypothesis.  Such repurposing happens all the time with engineering units that have fulfilled their primary purpose and have entered a second life as utility units.

By the way, as I mentioned above, LTA-8 used G&N system #602, of which we have the engineering drawings, including the drawings for AGC p/n 2003100-071.  That's how I knew, for example, that the p/n of module B11 was wrong.  By "engineering drawings", of course, I mean the mechanical drawings and the electrical schematics.  Other pertinent facts about G&N system #602 are that it would have used DSKY model 2003985-081 (whose drawings are also at the link just given) and that the AGC would have been loaded with the AURORA software.  We have a copy of AURORA 12, and you can view it in a variety of forms, including a pretty-printed transcription to source code.  Whether this is the exact version of the software is unclear, but it is a version that would undoubedly have functioned adequately in LTA-8.  A significant fact about AURORA is it includes a full suite of self-test software ... the last AGC software version to do so, since in later versions the self-test was greatly curtailed in order to save precious memory.  Thus AURORA was typically the software used during Project Apollo itself to check out the AGC.
