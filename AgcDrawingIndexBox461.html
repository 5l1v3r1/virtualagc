Further Information

Besides MIT/IL drawings, a few non-MIT drawings have gotten mixed into the collection over the course of time.  The index provides a few links to documents that were preserved as if they were drawings, but most links are to actual engineering drawings.  Hopefully, the list can be kept current on an ongoing basis as the number of available drawings increases.

The index is arranged by drawing number.  Most of these drawing numbers are 8-digit numbers.  Block I drawings generally have numbers like 10xxxxxx, while Block II drawings generally have numbers like 20xxxxxx, although other drawings (such as Specification Control Drawings, SCDs) are also mixed into that numbering system as 10xxxxxx numbers as well.

In the simplest case, a "drawing" is just a sheet of paper that has been scanned and is presented as a single scanned image.  However, there are more-complicated cases as well, in which case a single drawing number might be represented as several or even as many, many scanned images:

  • Drawings usually advance through several revisions, beginning with "-", and progressing through "A", "B, "C", and so on.  If there are more than "Z" revisions, then you might see revision codes like "AA", "AB", "AC", and so on.  For example, one drawing in the index goes up to revision "DK".  Some letters in the alphabet were not used for revisions ("I", "O", "Q", "X").
  • Sometimes a drawing has multiple physical pages, called "sheets".  A 3-sheet drawing, therefore, might appear in the index as three separate scans.
  • Most of our drawings were preserved as "microforms" attached to "aperture cards" ... which means that a photograph was taken of each sheet, and the photographic negatives were stored (and eventually scanned) in place of the original paper drawings.  But if the original sheet was physically larger than approximately an E-sized sheet of paper (44"×34"), it was too large to be captured by a single photograph.  Therefore, it was photographed instead as a set of 2, 3, or 4 "frames", with frame 1 usually being the leftmost frame.   In those cases each scanned image is really a frame of a sheet, rather than an entire frame.  Of course, today, we could digitally process a set of scanned frames, and instead produce a single image for each sheet.  Perhaps that will be done for our collection someday; presently, it has not been done for the drawings indexed here, though it has been done for a subset of them elsewhere on this site, when there has been a specific need to do so.
Most (but not all) of the scans in the collection were captured from aperture cards (held permanently at the U.S. National Archives).  Aperture cards are computer punch cards, into which a photographic negative has been attached.  The punch card itself encodes metadata about the negative, such as its title, the total number of sheets, the specific sheet number, the number of frames in the sheet, the specific frame number, and so on.  Among the metadata fields is a "type" fields, and the drawings themselves are of "type 01".  Sometimes a drawing is accompanied by "type 02" cards, which are generally (always?) titled PRODUCT CONFIGURATION AND ACCEPTANCE TEST REQUIREMENTS and may provide a lot of supplemental information such as expected voltage levels and waveforms that the electrical drawings don't provide.

It is currently an ongoing effort on my part to scan all of the boxes of aperture cards (nominally 2000 cards each) of MIT/IL drawings, though at present only a small part of it has been completed.  Where those complete scans have become available, they are the best places to look for drawings.  The miscellaneous index contains mostly alternate scans of subsets of the cards in the complete-box scans, but also contains a few hundred drawings missing from the National Archives' boxes.

Finally, note that the descriptive text in the "Link" column of the table isn't really very significant.  If the scan is simply a stand-alone file on this Virtual AGC Project website, the link text reads "drawing", while if it is instead a page within a larger batch of drawings at our Internet Archive site, then it is the page number within the specific batch of scans it originally belonged to.  There are no references to drawings (if any) that may exist at unaffiliated websites.   Regardless, just ignore the text of the link and click on it! 

By the way, if you should happen to download an image from the Internet Archive site by using your browser's "save image as" function, or if you try to download a PDF from there, you will probably get a very low-quality (though usually adequate) image rather then the full-quality scan.  To get the full-quality scan from our Internet Archive site, you will need to download the zipfile of JPEG200 (JP2) images instead of an individual image.  Some of the electrical drawings in the index have been manually transcribed into CAD files, using the open-source KiCad schematic-capture program.  Those transcriptions can be found at our GitHub site, but aren't explicitly linked in the index.
